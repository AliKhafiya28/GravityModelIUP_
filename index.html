Gravity Model Analysis of
Residential Location Attractiveness

A spatial-interaction model estimating where people live, work, and travel within Kecamatan Cerme — built to complement the Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) for residential area suitability, by translating accessibility and gravity-based potential into a Residential Attractiveness Index (RAI) for 288 grid zones.

Overview

What this analysis does

Kecamatan Cerme was divided into a 500 m analysis grid (288 zones). For each zone, the model estimates how many residential trips it generates (based on population) and how many it attracts (based on the mass of nearby destinations — services, roads, and built-up activity), then distributes trips between zones using a gravity equation weighted by travel cost along the real road network. The resulting accessibility, gravity-potential, and net-flow scores are combined into a single Residential Attractiveness Index (RAI) per zone — a direct, quantitative input to the broader MCDA residential suitability study.

Methodology

Gravity model formulation

The model follows the classic spatial-interaction (gravity) formulation, calibrated with an exponential travel-cost decay function and run on the actual Kecamatan Cerme road network.

// Trips between zone i (origin) and zone j (destination)
Tij  =  Viα · Wjβ · f(cij)
// Exponential travel-cost decay
f(cij)  =  exp(−λ · cij)

From flows to the Residential Attractiveness Index

Each zone's RAI combines three normalized (0–100) components derived from the gravity model run:

  1. Gravity potential (G_norm)Total inbound + outbound gravity interaction Tij for the zone, normalized — how strongly the zone is connected to population and activity elsewhere.
  2. Accessibility (A_norm)Inverse of average travel cost to other zones — how quickly a resident of this zone can reach the rest of Cerme.
  3. Environmental suitability (E_norm)Locally-derived suitability score (land cost / flood-risk proxies from the parallel MCDA layers), normalized.
  4. Generated vs. attracted trips (gen, att, net, ratio)Population-weighted trips generated by residents of the zone vs. trips attracted to it — used to flag "residential-leaning" vs. "hub-leaning" zones.

Two model versions were run: v1 used building-footprint-based mass for Vi; v2 (used throughout this site) apportions BPS desa-level population to each 500 m zone via dasymetric mapping for a more realistic population-weighted estimate.

Connection to the MCDA Study

How this feeds the residential suitability MCDA

The Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis for residential area suitability in Kecamatan Cerme combines several spatial criteria (land cost, flood risk, slope, land use, proximity to facilities, etc.) into a composite suitability score per zone. This gravity model contributes — and cross-checks — the accessibility and demand-side dimension of that score:

  1. Accessibility criterion (A_norm, RAI)Replaces a simple "distance to road" proxy with a network-based travel-cost measure that accounts for the whole road graph, not just nearest-link distance.
  2. Demand / population-pressure criterion (G_norm, Pop_zone)Gravity potential reflects how much residential and activity "mass" surrounds a zone — a forward-looking demand signal that complements static land-use criteria.
  3. Generation vs. attraction balance (gen/att/net/ratio)Zones where attraction > generation behave more like activity hubs (commercial/services); zones where generation > attraction behave more like dormitory/residential areas — useful for validating MCDA "residential" classifications.
  4. Infrastructure criterion (Main Routes, road network)The aggregated main-route flows highlight which corridors carry the most movement, helping prioritize infrastructure-related MCDA criteria (e.g. proximity to arterial roads).

In short: the MCDA answers "where is land physically/environmentally suitable for housing?", while the gravity model answers "where would housing actually be well-connected and in demand?" — RAI can be added as an additional weighted criterion, or used as an independent validation layer against the MCDA's final suitability map.

High-RAI zones cluster around Cerme Lor / Cerme Kidul / Betiting / Cagakagung — the existing central/sub-urban core with dense population and strong road connectivity. Low-RAI zones are concentrated in peripheral, sparsely-populated areas of Kandangan, Gedangkulud, Tambakberas, and Banjarsari, consistent with their distance from main routes and lower built-up density.
